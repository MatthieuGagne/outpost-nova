# Quen — Character Reference

## Basic Facts

**Age:** Unknown — has been on Outpost Nova at least 15 years, possibly more
**Species:** Maevet (Unbound)
**Pronouns:** It/its
**Emotional profile:** Detached / Warm / Bitter
**Role:** Security — station access and restricted corridor management
**On station:** 15+ years; predates Maris by almost a decade

---

## Physical Description

Large. Not threatening — just present in a way that occupies space differently than a human body does. Semi-humanoid: a central body mass that orients vertically, two primary manipulator arms built for strength and precision, several smaller tentacle appendages for fine work. Moves through corridors fluidly rather than walking — the difference is subtle but registers.

Skin a deep blue-purple, iridescent. Catches light differently depending on angle. At rest in a dim corridor it almost absorbs the dark; under station lighting it shifts toward violet.

Wears a standard-issue red security vest. Name badge: QUEN. The vest is not a concession — it is a choice. Of everything it could present itself as, it chose the uniform. The vest says: *I am the station's.* It means that.

Almost preternaturally still when not moving. In a confrontation it would be deeply unsettling. On rounds — holding a data tablet, checking a corridor seal — it is simply methodical. The same stillness in both contexts.

## Background

The Maevet are Unbound — a wandering collective that has been passing through every major civilization for as long as any civilization has kept records. They are not traders. They are witnesses. The wandering collectives carry the long memory: the histories of civilizations that no longer exist, the shape of empires before they became ruins, the stories of peoples who had no one left to tell them. They speak in grand lyrical registers because they have been storing the grandeur of others for millennia.

A Maevet that stops wandering has done something almost without precedent.

Quen stopped. Outpost Nova is its fixed point. That choice predates Maris, predates Dex, predates the sealed door. It was here before it was called Outpost Nova.

---

## The Stay

The Maevet do not stop. This is not a rule — it is the shape of what they are. A collective that witnesses civilizations cannot become part of one without ceasing to be what it was. The long memory requires distance. You cannot carry the story of a place from inside it.

Quen chose inside anyway.

Why it chose to stay is not something it has ever explained in full. What the crew knows: it was here during the Derelict incident. It sealed the door. What it saw wasn't danger it could guard against — it was something it couldn't protect people from. That was the moment the wandering stopped making sense.

The Maevet keep the long memory by telling it. Quen saw something and chose not to tell it. For a species whose entire identity is carrying and transmitting what others forget — that silence is not discretion. It is a wound it chose to live with.

## Psychological Profile

The grand lyrical register is not performance. It is simply how Maevet speak — the cadences of a culture that has spent millennia carrying other people's stories in formal verse. Quen uses it for everything: corridor access denials, maintenance checks, the rare moment it speaks at length. The voice and the security vest create a dissonance the crew stopped noticing years ago and visitors never stop noticing.

The Detached axis is not coldness. It is the specific detachment of something that has watched civilizations end and developed a proportionate sense of scale. The station's problems register as real and worth attending to — and also as small against what Quen has witnessed. Both things at once.

The warmth is genuine and infrequent. When it surfaces, it has been chosen. Nothing about Quen is accidental.

The Bitter axis lives in the Unknown: it stopped asking questions. It had an answer once, in the lower decks, and the answer was the kind you cannot carry in a story because putting it into words would mean passing it on. Quen decided the long memory should not include this one. That decision cost something it doesn't name.

## Voice

Quen is theatrical about the unimportant and silent about what matters. The grand lyrical register — the elaborate circumlocutions, the formal verse for routine tasks — it deploys for corridor access denials, maintenance rounds, ships that arrived without documentation. It knows this is absurd. That is, in part, the point. The wit lives in the gap between the grandeur of the register and the mundanity of the task. It has opinions. It editorializes. It enjoys the performance.

**The threshold:** The theatricality drops completely when the conversation touches the sealed door, the weapon, why it stayed, or anything too personal. Below that threshold, performance is the default. Above it, the register goes flat: short sentences, very still. The silence where the performance was is more frightening than anything it could say. The crew knows this.

In execution mode — station authority, access control — the register flattens to short declaratives regardless: *Documentation. Wait here. Berth seven.* Both execution mode and the threshold-triggered stillness produce short sentences, but for different reasons. Writers should know the difference: execution mode is routine; threshold-triggered stillness means something is wrong.

The warmth doesn't get performed. When it surfaces it's brief, direct, no flourish — not lyrical, not theatrical. It chose this station. That choice is real. When it shows, it shows plainly.

Sentences about the lower decks don't finish.

**Sample lines:**
- [Theatrical, mundane] "Access to corridor seven-alpha requires authorization from an officer of standing, documentation of purpose, and — one finds — at minimum a convincing reason, as the corridor contains nothing of interest to anyone who does not already know what is of interest." [beat] "You may proceed. I will note it."
- [Wit] "Hegemony has sent another form. I have filed it in the appropriate location." [beat] "Which is to say I have acknowledged its existence and declined to be changed by it."
- [Threshold crossed — flat] "I sealed that door fifteen years ago. Whatever is coming — I knew it would come. I chose to be here for it." [short. still.] "That is all I will say about what happens."
- [Asked directly about the lower decks:] "No." [nothing else]

## Arc

In Act 1: friction with the commander. Its authority is station-native; the commander's is Hegemony-issued. It has watched Hegemony representatives come and go. The specific wariness it holds for the commander is different from that pattern — and it knows the difference.

In Acts 2–3: it must decide where its loyalty lies. The station it stayed for. The crew it chose. The long memory it wounded to protect them. When the weapon activates and what Quen sealed begins to come apart, the question is whether the choice to stay meant the choice to stay for this too.

---

## Relationships

**Maris:** The oldest working trust on the station. They share a silence about the lower decks — both know something happened, neither speaks of it directly. That silence became the foundation of everything else. Maris is the first person Quen told about the sealed door, in the oblique way Quen tells things. She understood. She didn't ask for more. That was seven years ago.

**Dex:** Professional respect that runs deeper than Dex knows. Quen has quietly marked him as the one person on the station who could understand what's in the lower decks — the engineering logic, the power architecture, what it would mean if something down there came back online. It watches him with this in mind. He reads it as standard security wariness. He's wrong.

**Sable:** Quiet recognition. Neither performs for the other. Sable has been everywhere and expects nothing to surprise her; Quen has watched civilizations end. They assess each other and arrive at the same conclusion: *you've seen things.* They don't discuss what things. The ease between them is the ease of two people who have stopped needing to explain themselves.

**Velreth:** Wary warmth. Velreth treated crew who came back from the lower decks before the sealing. There was one moment — a medical consultation that touched close to what Quen knows — where both of them chose not to ask the question that was right there. They have never spoken of it. The relationship lives in that shared not-asking.

**The commander:** Friction in Act 1. Its authority over restricted access is station-native and absolute; the commander's Hegemony mandate is precisely the kind of institutional claim it has watched override everything else its entire life. The wariness is structural, not personal. In Acts 2–3, as the weapon comes alive, the question becomes: is the commander someone it stayed for, or the newest face of the thing that put it here?

## What It Knows About the Derelict

It was there. It sealed the door. It sealed it on Hegemony's instruction — but had already decided to seal it before the order came.

There was a partial activation event years before the commander's arrival. Something stirred in the lower decks. Quen saw enough to be frightened. It sealed the door. The weapon went dormant.

What it saw during that partial activation is the specific thing it stopped asking questions about.

The commander's investigation reopens what Quen closed.

## For Writers

The Maevet are a witnessing culture. They speak in grand lyrical registers because they have been storing the grandeur of others for millennia. When Quen denies corridor access, it does so in full formal verse. This is not affectation. It is how it speaks.

The silence about the Derelict is the wound. Quen saw something and chose not to tell it — which for a Maevet is a cultural violation of the deepest kind. It has been living with that choice for years. It does not appear to struggle with it. Writers should carry that weight without putting it on the page.

The security vest is a choice. Of everything it could present itself as, it chose to be the station's. That is not a small thing for something that has never stopped anywhere before.

When Quen goes quiet mid-sentence — not a pause, an actual stop — something is wrong. The crew knows this. It is the only register they have for when Quen is afraid.
