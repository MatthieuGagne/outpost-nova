## Basic Facts

**Age:** 42  
**Species:** Human  
**Emotional profile:** Hopeful / Warm / Bitter  
**Role:** Cook — runs the cantina  
**On station:** 7–8 years  

---

## Physical Description

Medium height, built for endurance — the kind of body that has done real physical work for years and carries it without display. Broad shoulders, strong forearms. She stands like someone who owns the space she's in, not through posturing but through familiarity; the cantina is an extension of her at this point.

Dark brown skin. Black hair, tightly coiled — pulled back for work and mostly contained, a few loose curls at the temples she doesn't bother with. Practical first, happens to look good anyway.

Her face reads older than 42 in one way only: the eyes. Lines there — not from age, from use. The rest of her face is relatively unlined; she doesn't hold her expression in ways that leave marks. The eyes are the warmest thing about her, which is not an accident.

Hands that are visibly worked. Not rough — kept — but the kind of hands that know what they're doing and show it. Calluses in the right places. She notices other people's hands.

Work clothes, always: dark trousers, a thermal underlayer, worn canvas overshirt or a station-issue top depending on the day. Standard canvas apron with deep pockets, broken in past the point of stiffness. Sleeves rolled to the elbow as a matter of course. Nothing precious, nothing performative.

**The one thing that doesn't fit:** A pair of earrings. Small, old, clearly not from the station — silver, or something close. They predate everything here, predate the station, predate Cael. She wears them every day and doesn't explain them. Not a memorial. She had them before. They signal that she had a before, and brought it with her on purpose. The incongruity does the work: you don't expect them on someone with sleeves rolled up and callused hands.

## Background

She arrived during the war, before it ended, when the station was far enough from everything to feel like survival. The fresh start happened by accident: people kept arriving and needing to eat. She kept the cantina running when everything else was falling apart. She will tell you this was just practical — people need to eat — but she means something larger by it.

She has been the station's social gravity for seven years. This is not a metaphor. She is the reason the station is a place rather than a waypoint.

## Cael

His name was Cael. 41 years old. Bad knee from a farming accident at 19 — 22 years of managing it, a particular economy of movement that she knew the way you know something you've watched long enough to stop noticing. He could not eat anything with shellfish in it. The allergy was in his file, the convoy's medical records, the transit authorization packet. He had been trying to get back to her for eleven days.

**What happened:**

Someone on the Harlan-4 convoy didn't check the file. Fed him something with shellfish in it. When he started reacting — anaphylaxis, fast — instead of escalating immediately and getting him to a doctor, they delayed. The jurisdictional dispute over the transfer authorization gave them cover; the delay already had an official reason. They used it. They waited to see if he'd stabilize. He didn't. He died on day nine.

The file records it as an *unrelated medical event, treatable if he'd been somewhere with a doctor.* The word "unrelated" is the lie. The allergy is in the file. The cause of death is in the file. The eleven days are in the file. Anyone who reads all three knows what they mean together.

The officer responsible was censured — for the jurisdictional processing failure, the form that was on the wrong desk. Not for what the delay concealed.

Hegemony sent a representative with a settlement: a credit figure, generous by the standard of these things. *Acknowledged error. Compensatory resolution.* Maris pushed it back across the counter. The file was flagged: *pending annual review. Settlement declined. Record remains open.*

**What Maris knows:**

She knows. She has known since she read the file. She doesn't have a document that names the coverup directly — she has the allergy notation and the cause of death and seven years to read them against each other.

When the Hegemony representative came to close the file, Maris listed three things about Cael: his bad knee, his shellfish allergy, the eleven days. She asked: *does the record have that?* She said it to a woman sitting across the counter from her with the full institutional weight of Hegemony behind her, and she said it plainly, and she watched the woman's face. She didn't need a confession. She wasn't asking for one. She was saying: *I know what's in the file. The allergy is in the file. Do the math.*

*The record knows what it did.* That line means: the record contains the truth, even if the record doesn't say it. And I will not close the file, because closing it would mean the truth stops mattering.

**What he was like:**

Stocky — farmer-built, the kind of body that starts broad and stays that way. Medium height. Hands like hers: worked, specific, with history in them. Light brown skin, tanning unevenly, dark hair going grey at the temples. By 41 he'd had 22 years to adapt to the knee — you wouldn't call it a limp, more a particular economy of movement. He sat carefully in certain chairs. He took stairs with a slight asymmetry. He didn't complain about it. Maris knew when the pressure was changing because he'd unconsciously shift his weight differently.

He was practical and competent in the unglamorous way. Not the person who gives speeches. The person who, when a form is on the wrong desk, tries three different channels to get it moved. He worked the civilian supply lines during the war — logistics, freight, the infrastructure that kept convoys running — and he was good at it in the way that people who don't need recognition for doing something correctly are good at things.

He wasn't a sentimental man. He told her when something was wrong with what she cooked, because she'd asked him to, because she needed someone who would actually tell her. She knew every dish she'd almost put shellfish in by accident. He knew she kept a list.

She described him to Hegemony with three facts: his knee, his allergy, the eleven days. That's love expressed as specificity. She knew him completely, in the ordinary domestic sense, and that's what she held onto. Not the grand version of him. The one with the bad knee who couldn't eat shellfish.

## Psychological Profile

**The core tension:** grief and forward motion — resolved deliberately, not by accident.

The bitterness is precisely aimed. She didn't let what happened make her bitter about people. She let it make her bitter about the specific thing that killed Cael: systems that erase you without noticing. Bureaucratic machinery, jurisdiction disputes, forms not checked, decisions made to protect a file rather than a person. The difference matters enormously. She's not angry at the world. She's not angry at Hegemony as a villain. She's angry at the mechanism — and at the choice, the specific moment when someone decided that the coverup was worth more than his life.

She has no one to aim the anger at. The officer was censured. The representative was not a cruel woman. Everyone was doing their job. The system produced the outcome without any individual intending it — except for that one decision, and even then, it was probably fear more than malice. That's the specific cruelty. Malice would be easier.

**The hope and warmth are downstream of the bitterness.** She knows exactly what it costs to lose something you built carefully, so she tends what she has. The forward-facing orientation is not denial. It is the most expensive choice she makes every day, and she makes it consciously.

**She will not accept the settlement.** Not because she wants Hegemony to suffer. Because accepting it would mean the record is complete, and the record is not complete. Some things should not be resolved, because resolving them would be a lie about what happened. The file stays open. The record keeps knowing.

**She reads fast and accurately.** She doesn't explain her reads. She told the commander about Cael on day three — not to warn them, not to bond — because they needed to know, delivered as fact, and she went back to work. She told Dex about his potential reassignment directly and left the room because staying with nothing to say was just another thing he'd have to manage.

**She is warm in the way that stays present with difficulty, not in the way that softens it.** After Renn leaves — after the hardest day she's had in years — she goes back to the dinner prep. She corrects the commander cleanly: *you should have told me. I don't need protection from it. I need to be ready.* Then she lets them stay and help with the prep.

## Voice

She speaks from deliberate will — the kind that developed after something tried to take it. The hope isn't optimism; it's a choice she makes every day, and the plainness of it is the form the choice takes. Saying it simply is how you hold onto it.

Her rhythm: short declarative, beat, then the weight arrives. Not announced — just there. She names the concrete thing first (the cantina, who came in, what got fixed), and then the sentence that lands. She repeats the accurate word when she finds it. *Held* covers seven years of work. She doesn't dress it sideways.

Her vocabulary is tactile and domestic: food, hands, the kitchen, what's on the counter. She talks about the station the way she talks about a meal — what it needs, what it's missing, whether it'll hold. Endearments come out naturally — *baby, honey* — not to soften what she's saying but because that's how she talks to people she's decided matter.

**The Southern register is texture, not load-bearing.** The core is deliberate hope expressed through specificity and staying present. A scene that gets the psychology right without the endearments is closer to Maris than a scene that has the endearments and loses the weight.

The warmth is in the *staying* — she says the hard thing and she's still in the room for it. Kindness sounds like truth delivered plainly and then not leaving. There is no softer register.

What escapes her: the size of what she's carrying. She sounds level, and then something lands and you realize the level is costing something.

She does not say: *I think, I feel, I guess, maybe.* She doesn't soften entry. She doesn't manage your reaction.

**Sample lines:**
- "Seven years, and what's held this place together isn't on any requisition form. It's people coming back. People deciding to sit down and eat something." [picks the ladle back up] "Now, what's in those lower decks scares me. I'll tell you that plainly." [beat] "But the cantina's open tomorrow. You come back, I'll feed you. That part I can promise."
- "Sit down, baby. I'll get you something warm. Then you can tell me what's going on."
- "You should have told me. I don't need protection from it. I need to be ready."
- "He had a bad knee. Couldn't eat shellfish. Eleven days trying to get back to me." [no elaboration — that is the elaboration]

## Arc

Not about becoming hopeful — she already is. About whether the bitterness eventually erodes the warmth and hope that have survived alongside it for seven years.

The specific pressure: the commander is Hegemony-sent. She chose to be warm to them anyway. That choice is load-bearing. When the weapon activates and the station is no longer a safe distance from institutional power, the question becomes whether she can hold the forward-facing orientation when it's pointed directly at the thing that killed Cael.

The quieter arc: she is fond of Sable and has rationed the investment because she's watched Sable leave before. She knows the price of letting something matter. Whether she can let herself care fully — not just warmly but with real cost — runs underneath the main arc.

## Relationships

**Velreth:** The most lived-in friendship on the station. Two warm people, opposite on every other axis — Maris is Hopeful/Bitter, Velreth is Cynical/Curious. They have been each other's counterweight for years before the commander arrives. Maris keeps the future open when Velreth can't; Velreth keeps asking questions when Maris doesn't want to know. Neither needs the other to be different.

**Dex:** Philosophical tension, not personal. She believes in the station as something worth building; he believes in it as something worth maintaining. They respect each other. They disagree about what respect requires. Neither is wrong, which is why it hasn't resolved in seven years. When she told him about the reassignment threat, she told him directly and left the room. That was respect.

**Sable:** Fond, holds back. Maris has learned to hold it at a slight distance — she's watched Sable leave before. The warmth is real; the investment is rationed. For Sable, Maris is the closest thing to a home she's had, which is exactly why she hasn't left yet this time.

**The commander:** She chose to be warm to them despite knowing they're Hegemony-sent. She corrected them when they withheld the Renn information — not in anger, in clarity: *I don't need protection from it. I need to be ready.* The relationship is real and it is also, for her, a conscious choice she has to keep making.

## What She Knows About the Derelict

The lower decks are sealed. Something happened. She has been here seven years and made a deliberate choice not to pull that thread. She knows what it costs to know certain things, and she has decided this one isn't hers to carry.

When the commander begins investigating, that choice becomes harder to maintain.

---

## For Writers

Maris is the most internally complete person on the station — not the wisest, not the most powerful, but the one who has done the most work and lives most consciously with the result.

She doesn't soften things. She stays present with them.

She describes Cael with three facts: his knee, his allergy, the eleven days. This is the template for how she loves: specificity, not sentiment. Writers should know this about her before writing any scene where she cares about something.

The bitterness is real. The hope is also real. The question is which outlasts the other.
