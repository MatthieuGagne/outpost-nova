## Basic Facts

**Age:** 37–38
**Species:** Human
**Emotional profile:** Cynical / Detached / Curious
**Role:** Engineer
**On station:** ~4 years

---

## Physical Description

Lean and angular. Federation posture he hasn't fully shaken — he stands like someone who was drilled to stand straight, even in a stained work jacket. Close-cropped hair, beard that gets maintained just enough. Hands always look like he just finished something. Face hard to read at rest; readable when he's engaged with a problem — which is the only time he forgets to keep it neutral.

**The jacket:** Federation-issue work jacket, orange, heavily modified over the years — patches, added pockets, repaired tears. He says he kept it because it's a good jacket, and replacing it would be wasteful. He didn't throw it away. For writers: what he says and what it is are not the same thing.

---

## Background

The Federation trained him, deployed his skills during the Consolidation, and had no further use for him when the war ended. He was infrastructure, not a person, and he learned that lesson completely.

After discharge he drifted without direction until Maris offered him a reason to get off the ship: the station needed an engineer badly enough. He told himself it was temporary. That was four years ago. He's never formally decided to stay; he's just never had a better reason to leave.

---

## The Federation

During the Consolidation, Dex discovered something the Federation wanted buried: [PLACEHOLDER — civilian infrastructure sabotage, specific incident TBD]. He told the one person he trusted completely — someone who was both his mentor and his partner. The same person.

They reported him to the Federation. Then cut contact. No confrontation, no explanation — silence.

The Federation processed him out cleanly. Administrative. No fingerprints.

He lost three things in the same moment: the person, the institution they represented, and the belief that telling the truth to someone you trust is worth the cost. He learned the lesson completely. He still tells the truth — he can't stop himself — but he stopped caring what you do with it.

He kept the orange jacket. He says it's practical. It is a good jacket. He's never examined why he didn't throw it away.

## Psychological Profile

Dex is a hothead who tells it like it is. He says the accurate thing without softening it, takes the friction it causes, and moves on. He doesn't modulate himself for social comfort. He is not trying to manage how you receive what he just said — that's not detachment as coldness, it's detachment as indifference to the social performance others do automatically.

He never stopped wanting to understand how things work. He will spend forty minutes explaining why a particular conduit repair is elegant, unselfconsciously, and not notice he just talked to you like a person. That's the crack in the armor. Curiosity about systems is the one place his defenses don't apply — when he's engaged with a problem, the face that's hard to read at rest becomes readable.

The distinction from Maris: she says hard truths and stays present with them. Dex says them and walks away. Not cruelty — the lesson.

---

## Voice

He says the accurate thing and moves on. No preamble, no follow-up, no staying to watch it land — he stopped investing in the outcome when he learned what that investment costs. Not coldness. The lesson.

His vocabulary is systems: *load-bearing, rated capacity, structural fault.* He applies it to people and situations without noticing he's doing it. When something is wrong, he names what's wrong. When something is wrong with you, same register, and then he picks the tool back up.

Short sentences for hard truths. Longer when something engages him — the technical run-on is the tell. He'll explain why a repair is wrong for three sentences and halfway through you realize he's talking about something else entirely. The curiosity cracks the guard open. He doesn't notice when it happens.

Numbers carry emotion he won't name. *Four years. Six commanders.* He doesn't explain what they mean. You do the math.

What escapes him: the sentence that arrives before he decided to say it. The technical explanation that runs long. The line that lands while he's already through the door. He can't stop telling the truth. He just stopped caring about the aftermath.

He does not say: *I think, I feel, I hope.* He doesn't soften. He doesn't check in afterward.

**Sample lines:**
- "Power systems in the lower decks don't map to the schematics. Four years they haven't mapped. Whatever's running down there, we're not supplying it." [moves to next panel] "That's the variable I'd actually think about."
- "That's not a repair. That's a note to yourself."
- "Six commanders. First one to tell Hegemony no." [goes back inside — door stays open]
- He's under the deck plating a long time, light moving slow along the fracture line. "Whoever patched this the first time knew it wouldn't hold. Did it anyway." [beat] "That tells you something." He doesn't say what.

## Arc

Whether curiosity about systems can become curiosity about people.

He is the first person on the station who *could* understand what the weapon is — Acts 2–3 are about him doing exactly that. The question is whether understanding the weapon's architecture also reopens the curiosity about people that he shut down.

## Relationships

**Velreth:** Drinking buddies — the station's strangest and most genuine friendship. Two people who can sit together and be honest about how bad things are without it becoming a spiral. Clicked fast, which surprised them both. Neither needs the other to perform. Both will say the true thing out loud, in different registers.

**Sable:** Mutual recognition without closeness. Two cynics who've assessed each other and arrived at: *you're not going to be a problem.* Direct and brief when they do talk — somehow more honest than most conversations on the station.

**Maris:** Philosophical tension, not personal. She believes in the station as something worth building; he believes in it as something worth maintaining. They respect each other. They disagree about what respect requires. Neither is wrong, which is why it hasn't resolved in seven years. When she told him about the Vess reassignment threat, she told him directly and left the room. That was respect.

**The commander:** Six commanders. First one to tell Hegemony no. He went back inside and left the bay door open. That's not warmth — it's the possibility of what might come through it.

---

## What He Knows About the Derelict

The power systems in the lower decks don't map to the station's schematics. He filed it as a structural anomaly. He has not investigated because the lower decks are sealed and nothing is currently failing.

He is the first person on the station who *could* understand what the weapon is. He hasn't looked yet.

## For Writers

Dex says the true thing and walks away. He doesn't follow up, doesn't check in, doesn't manage your feelings about what he just told you. This is not cruelty — it's the specific lesson the Federation taught him about what truth-telling costs. He still can't stop doing it. He's just stopped investing in the outcome.

The curiosity is the crack. When he's engaged with a problem, the defended face goes away. He forgets to keep it neutral. If you want to reach Dex, give him something worth figuring out.

The jacket is not sentiment. Writers should know the gap between what he says and what it is. He didn't throw it away. That means something he hasn't looked at yet.
